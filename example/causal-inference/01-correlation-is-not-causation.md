# The Causation You Assume Is Probably Just Coincidence

You make causal judgments every day. "This video went viral because the opening was catchy." "I feel terrible today because I didn't sleep well last night."

But how many of these are real cause-and-effect, and how many are just stories your brain invented?

Judea Pearl said: **Humanity's biggest cognitive bug is confusing "happened together" with "caused."**

---

## Correlation vs Causation: An Example You'll Definitely Get Wrong

**Data**: In months when ice cream sales are highest, drowning deaths are also highest.

Your first reaction might be: eating ice cream causes drowning? Or drowning makes people want ice cream?

Neither. The real reason is **summer** — when it's hot, people buy more ice cream AND swim more. There's zero causal relationship between ice cream and drowning. They're both driven by a third factor (temperature).

This is called **spurious correlation**. Two things appearing together doesn't mean one caused the other.

---

## Why Your Brain Is Hardwired to Get This Wrong

Daniel Kahneman's research found that your brain has a factory setting called **narrative fallacy** — when we see two things happen in sequence, we automatically invent a causal story to connect them.

This isn't because you're dumb. In primitive societies, "heard rustling in bushes → assume tiger → run" kept you alive better than "let me carefully analyze the statistical probability of this sound." Your brain was designed by evolution to prefer false causation over missed threats.

**But in modern life, this factory setting hurts you:**

- You posted a video and the metrics exploded. You think it's because of the title. But maybe the platform's traffic pool was just bigger that day.
- You didn't post for three days and didn't lose followers. You think skipping is fine. But maybe the data just hasn't reflected the impact yet.
- You feel like "building a system made me improve," but maybe you improved because you posted more videos, and the system had nothing to do with it.

Every wrong causal judgment wastes your attention — you spend effort on things that aren't the real cause.

## Judea Pearl's Causal Ladder: Three Levels of Thinking

Pearl is a Turing Award winner who spent his life researching one question: **How can machines (and humans) actually distinguish causation?**

His answer is a three-level ladder. Most people stay on level one their entire lives.

### Level 1: Seeing (Association)

> "I see X and Y appearing together."

This is pure observation. Ice cream sales and drowning rates rise together. Videos with high save rates tend to have longer titles.

**This level only tells you correlation, not causation.**

90% of your daily judgments stop here.

### Level 2: Doing (Intervention)

> "If I actively do X, will Y happen?"

This is experimentation. Not observing "videos with long titles get more saves," but: **I deliberately make my next video's title longer and see if the save rate actually changes.**

Key difference: Level 1 is passively looking at data. Level 2 is actively changing one variable, controlling everything else, and checking if the result changes.

**This level is where causation lives. Because you intervened.**

### Level 3: Imagining (Counterfactual)

> "If I hadn't done X, what would have happened?"

This is counterfactual thinking. "If I hadn't posted that video yesterday, what would my follower count be today?" You can never directly verify this (because you can't rewind time), but you can approximate the answer through logical reasoning.

**This is the highest level of causal reasoning.**
